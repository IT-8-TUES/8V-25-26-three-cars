Audi Quattro

1980 — 1991  |  The car that rewrote the rules

Overview

The Audi Quattro — internally known as the Typ 85 — is the car that single-handedly changed what people thought all-wheel drive could do. Before 1980, AWD was associated with heavy, slow off-road trucks. Audi's engineers, led by Jörg Bensinger and Walter Treser, looked at that assumption and decided to prove it completely wrong. The result was a turbocharged, permanently driven four-wheel-drive sports coupé that became a legend the moment it appeared at the Geneva Motor Show in March 1980.

The name "Quattro" — Italian for "four" — referred to the four driven wheels, and it would go on to become one of the most famous badges in automotive history. Production ran from 1980 to 1991, with 11,452 units built in total. Every single one was hand-assembled at the Audi factory in Ingolstadt, Germany.

The Rally Revolution

The Quattro's impact on motorsport was immediate and total. When Hannu Mikkola entered the 1981 Jänner Rally in Austria, other drivers genuinely thought Audi had made a mistake — AWD in a rally car? It was considered a gimmick. Mikkola won by nearly half an hour. The paddock went silent.

Michèle Mouton then became the first woman to win a World Rally Championship round, driving a Quattro to victory at the San Remo Rally in 1981. She went on to finish runner-up in the 1982 WRC Drivers' Championship — still the best result by a female driver in the history of the series. Audi won the Manufacturers' Championship in 1982 and 1984. Walter Röhrl, one of the greatest rally drivers who ever lived, called it the most important car he ever drove.

The Group B era brought the Quattro Sport — a shortened, even more extreme version with 480 horsepower and a 0–100 km/h time of 3.1 seconds. In the mid-1980s. That number still sounds fast today.

Engineering

The key to the Quattro was the transfer of a four-wheel-drive system originally developed for the Iltis military vehicle into a road car. The system used three differentials — front, centre, and rear — all lockable, giving the driver full control over how power was distributed. Combined with a turbocharged five-cylinder engine producing 200 hp in standard form, the result was traction that no rear-wheel-drive car could match in slippery conditions.

The five-cylinder engine itself was unusual — between a four and a six in terms of smoothness, with a distinctive sound that Audi enthusiasts still chase today. Over the production run it grew from 2.1 to 2.2 litres and eventually produced up to 220 hp in road form.

Legacy

The Quattro's legacy cannot be overstated. Every AWD performance car that exists today — the Subaru Impreza WRX, the Mitsubishi Lancer Evolution, the Porsche 911 Carrera 4, the BMW M5 xDrive — all of them exist because Audi proved in 1980 that AWD and performance were not opposites. The Quattro didn't just win rallies. It rewrote an entire chapter of automotive engineering.

In 2004, Sports Car International named it the fourth greatest sports car of the 1980s. In numerous polls of automotive journalists, it consistently appears among the most important cars ever built — not just the most important Audis, the most important cars, full stop.
